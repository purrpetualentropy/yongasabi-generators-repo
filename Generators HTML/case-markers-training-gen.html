A note about repeating markers

This comes up in the grammar document, but it's possible for some case markers, like the dative 'im / m or the locative -wa, to show up multiple times in a sentence, performing different functions.

pachmigaega chomugwa hanoltakwa sawil - At night, Survivor sings at the hunting grounds.
hanitaega sattokubim mulkom go - Hunter is going to the market for Artificer.

Typically in natural Yongasabi, the word order in sentences can be moved around to prioritise information differently, as words closer to the verb are considered more important.

sattokubiga lamlansang kip'o damkasa - Artificer is cleaning the shelter with Rivulet. Here the sentence is organised so that the direct object kip holds more emphasis than the instrumental.
sattokubiga kip'o lamlansang damkasa - Artificer, with Rivulet, is cleaning the shelter. Here the sentence is organised so that the instrumental lamlan holds more emphasis than the direct object.
(When it comes to truly placing emphasis on something it's more common to use the focus marker hei, and depending on the intended meaning it may be more appropriate.)

However, in this generator, sentences will always follow a specific word order, so you can tell what function a marker is performing based on its location in a sentence. That order is as follows:

Vocative
Subject
Timing
Location
Ablative
Instrumental
Indirect object
Destination
Direct object
Verb

If the usage is ambiguous, such as in hanitaega sattokubim go - where Artificer could be the indirect object or the destination - you can just decide on your own. It doesn't necessarily matter that your interpretation agrees with what the generator has labelled this word, just that you understand the meaning of the sentence as you read it. If you can understand hanitaega sattokubim go both as "Hunter is going on Artificer's behalf" and "Hunter is going to Artificer", I'd consider that a success.
